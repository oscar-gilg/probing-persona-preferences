# Introduction

**Personas.** Modern LLMs *role-play*: a single model enacts different characters — personas, configurations of tone, values, and choices — depending on system prompt, conversation, or fine-tuning. Early work frames LLMs accordingly: as *agent models* that infer and simulate the producer of a text (Andreas 2022), as *simulators* running one of many possible simulacra (Janus 2022), and as role-players whose dialogue is best read as an enacted character rather than a unified agent (Shanahan 2023). More recently, persona-modulating behaviours have been linked to linear structure in activation space: Chen et al. (2025) extract single *persona vectors* for traits like evil, sycophancy, and hallucination propensity that predict and steer behaviour, and Lu et al. (2026) isolate an "Assistant Axis" inside the default chat model. Personas are not just surface style — they have correlates at the level of internal representations.

**The persona-selection model.** Marks et al. (2026) consolidate this line of work into the *persona-selection model* (PSM). They formalise the proposal that pretraining yields a simulator of many characters and post-training elicits and refines one of them — the *Assistant* — into the persona users interact with; and they update the picture in light of recent empirical findings, surveying behavioural, generalisation, and interpretability evidence consistent with PSM. PSM tells us *that* LLMs are persona-enactors. It leaves open how exhaustive that picture is — whether there are sources of agency outside the active persona — and, separately, *how* personas are realised in the network at inference: whether qualitatively different personas share representational machinery, or run on largely separate circuits.

**Two open questions.** The questions PSM leaves open pick out two largely independent axes:

- **Q1 (agency).** Are there behavioural signs of an entity beyond the active persona — some persona-independent agency that persists across whichever character the model is enacting (the "masked Shoggoth" picture) — or is the network well-described as a pure persona-enactor?
- **Q2 (representation).** How are personas implemented? Do qualitatively different personas share representational machinery, with persona-specific behaviour falling out of different settings of shared parameters, or does each persona run on its own circuitry?

Q1 and Q2 are largely independent: a model could share circuitry across personas without any extra agency, or run separate persona circuits with an outer agent on top. Q1 is the higher-stakes question for AI safety and welfare; Q2 is the more methodologically tractable. This paper is principally about Q2 — but the answer constrains Q1, since a single mechanism that flips sign with the active persona is hard to reconcile with a stable persona-independent agent underneath.

**Preferences as a window onto persona representations.** Among the ways personas differ, what they *prefer* is one of the most consequential, and a natural locus for AI welfare (Long 2024). Finding an internal representation that encodes preferences under one persona lets us ask whether the same representation does the work under others — a concrete instance of Q2, with bearing on Q1.

**Contributions.** **A single linear direction in Gemma-3-27B's residual stream predicts and steers preferences, and does so across qualitatively different personas.**

1. **A linear probe predicts and causally steers Gemma-3-27B's revealed preferences.**
   - The probe predicts held-out preferences within-topic and across topics, beating a sentence-transformer content baseline.
   - Steering along the probe direction near-completely controls pairwise task choice, while a random direction at the same magnitude has no effect.
2. **The same direction works across qualitatively different personas.**
   - A probe trained on the default Assistant transfers as a classifier *and* as a steering vector to prompted personas with partially or fully inverted overt values (villain, sadist) and to character-fine-tuned variants.
   - Steering amplifies whichever persona is active rather than pulling toward a fixed attractor: under the sadist, + means *more* sadist; under the default, + has no measurable effect on sadism.
   - The probe also tracks prompt-induced preference shifts under persona system prompts, single-sentence biographical context, and role-played framings of harm, truth, and politics.
3. **These findings constrain persona-selection theory and bear on AI safety (see Discussion).**
   - On Q2, our results place LLMs in the shared-circuitry region.
   - A Shoggoth view combined with shared circuitry would predict a persona-independent attractor underneath each persona's probe; we do not find one — probes resemble the persona they were trained on rather than any fixed attractor (App.: probe bias toward training persona vs default assistant).
   - Understanding what drives model choices matters as LLMs are deployed in dilemmas and high-stakes settings; our work contributes a single evaluative vector that is predictive and causal, and shows that this vector is not unique — comparable in-distribution probes can be fit on linearly independent directions, though the *generalising* signal is effectively rank-1 (App.: preference direction uniqueness).
   - The same direction — trained on preferences, not refusal — partially overrides refusal guardrails at small steering coefficients, indicating representational overlap that neither training objective predicts.

**Related work in brief.** Anthropic's *Emotion Concepts and their Function in a Large Language Model* (Anthropic 2026) identifies ~171 emotion-concept vectors via SAEs in the default Assistant. We differ in (a) training a supervised probe on revealed preferences, sharper in the preference regime, and (b) centring on persona-relative generalisation. # Discussion

**Persona representations are shared (Q2).** A probe trained on the default Assistant transfers to other personas as both classifier and steering vector (§3); in the open-ended setting it amplifies whichever persona is active rather than imposing the default (§3); and it tracks prompt-induced shifts under role-played harm, truth, and politics framings (§4). To the extent that preference is a representative dimension on which personas differ, this places the model in the shared-circuitry region of the spectrum — the same machinery is read and written by qualitatively different personas.

**There is no persona-independent attractor underneath (Q1).** A Shoggoth view combined with shared circuitry predicts a fixed persona-independent preference profile underneath each persona's probe — the residual attractor that should emerge as we fit probes persona by persona. We do not find one: per-persona probes resemble the persona they were trained on rather than any default attractor (App.: probe bias toward training persona vs default assistant). Steering tells the same story — the + direction means different things in different personas (under the sadist, + is more sadist; under the default, + has no measurable effect on sadism). The representations are *persona-instrumental*: each persona reads and writes them in its own terms. This is evidence against Shoggoth-style accounts of LLM agency, though not a knockout — a Shoggoth could act through non-preference dimensions, and other operationalisations remain open.

**Implication for interpretability: features are persona-instrumental.** Our preference direction transfers across personas as a *mechanism*, not as a fixed content direction — extending Lampinen et al. (2026) from general character-tracking features to preference. A linear feature that tracks behaviour under one persona can carry opposite or absent content under another, so single-persona probing over-indexes on persona-instrumental features and under-reports on what (if anything) is persona-invariant.

**Implications for AI safety: building an understanding of what drives model choices.** What drives a model's choice in a given context is a first-order question for AI safety, as LLMs are deployed into dilemmas and high-stakes settings. The findings of this paper take a step in that direction: a single linear direction in residual-stream activations that is predictive of revealed preferences (§2) and causal under steering, that does the same job across qualitatively different personas (§3), and that tracks the model's preference shifts under role-played harm, truth, and politics framings (§4). As a concrete safety consequence, the same direction — trained on preference, not refusal — partially overrides refusal guardrails at small steering coefficients (App.: causal evaluative evidence), evidence that preference and refusal share more representational machinery than their training objectives would predict.

**Limitations.**

- **Effects are not black-and-white.** The probe predicts held-out preferences with high but imperfect correlation, and contrastive steering shifts pairwise choice over a wide range but not deterministically. The linear-direction picture captures most of the signal but leaves a substantive residual.
- **Personas are mostly prompt-based.** Cross-persona transfer is established under system-prompted personas; weight-level replication is restricted to the OpenCharacter LoRA variants (App.: evaluative evidence), with replication on a wider set of finetuned personas left to future work.
- **We are not claiming this is the preference direction.** In-distribution decoding admits multiple linearly independent preference probes (App.: preference direction uniqueness); we identify a preference-encoding direction within a multi-dimensional subspace, not the only one. The cross-topic generalising signal is, however, effectively rank-1, so for transfer-relevant questions the single-direction picture is the right one. *[Note: the preference-direction-uniqueness appendix is a placeholder — content will be expanded once the writeup lands.]*
