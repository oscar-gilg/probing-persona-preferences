\begin{abstract}
Personas in Gemma-3-27B share a single linear preference direction: a probe trained on the default assistant's revealed pairwise preferences transfers, both as a classifier and as a steering vector, to personas whose overt values are partially or fully inverted (villain, sadist), and to character-fine-tuned variants. The direction is evaluative rather than descriptive: it tracks preference shifts induced by system prompts, single-sentence biographical context, and role-played harm and truth framings at $r \approx \abstractTargetedTasksRApproximation$ on targeted tasks, whereas a sentence-transformer content baseline does not. It also discriminates evaluative content at multiple token positions, from the final prompt token to task-averaged activations and the turn boundary, consistent with the model compiling an evaluative summary and re-reading it across the forward pass. These findings update against persona-independent (``Shoggoth'') accounts of LLM agency, and bear on AI welfare, since evaluative representations are functionally central in theories of moral patiency, and on AI safety, since the same direction overrides refusal guardrails.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\paragraph{Personas.}
Modern LLMs \emph{role-play}: a single model enacts different characters --- personas, character-like configurations of tone, values, and choices --- depending on system prompt, conversation, or fine-tuning. A line of early work frames LLMs accordingly: as \emph{agent models} that infer and simulate the producer of a text~\citep{andreas2022agentmodels}, as \emph{simulators} running one of many possible simulacra~\citep{janus2022simulators}, and as role-players whose dialogue is best read as an enacted character rather than a unified agent~\citep{shanahan2023roleplay}. Recent work has made this quantitative: \citet{chen2025personavectors} extract persona vectors for traits like evil, sycophancy, and hallucination propensity and use them to predict and steer behaviour; \citet{lu2026assistantaxis} isolate an ``Assistant Axis'' inside the default chat model. A persona is more than surface style --- it has structure at the level of internal representations.

\paragraph{The persona-selection model.}
\citet{marks2026personaselection} consolidate this into the \emph{persona-selection model} (PSM): pretraining yields a simulator of many characters, and post-training refines one --- the \emph{Assistant} --- into the persona users interact with. PSM tells us \emph{that} LLMs are persona-enactors; it leaves open \emph{how} this is realized at inference. A spectrum of mechanistic pictures is compatible:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Operating-system view.} All agency lives in the active persona.
\item \textbf{Router view.} The base model's only role is selecting which persona runs.
\item \textbf{Shoggoth view.} The base model has its own agency that shapes outputs from underneath.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Preferences as a test bed.}
What separates personas, more than anything else, is what they \emph{prefer} --- a sadist and a helpful assistant differ not in tone but in which situations they welcome and which they reject. If we can find the representation that encodes preferences, we can ask directly \emph{whose} preferences it encodes.

\paragraph{Two open questions.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Q1 (agency).} Are there persona-independent preferences beneath the persona --- a fixed layer (``masked Shoggoth'') that every persona sits on top of?
\item \textbf{Q2 (representation).} Do personas share preference circuitry, or does each persona carry its own?
\end{itemize}
Concretely, these pick out two predictions for what a preference-encoding direction could look like: \emph{(i)} a vector that tracks the \emph{model's} preferences across any persona it runs (Shoggoth-compatible); or \emph{(ii)} a vector that tracks whichever persona is currently active --- shared mechanism, persona-modulated readout.

\paragraph{Main result.}
\textbf{A single linear direction in Gemma-3-27B's residual stream predicts and steers preferences, and does so across qualitatively different personas.} The probe predicts held-out revealed preferences at $r = \positionSweepGemmaEndofturnBestR$ within-topic and pooled $r = \gemmaProbeCrossTopicPooledR$ cross-topic, and causally flips pairwise task choices at $P \geq \contrastivePChoseSteeredAtLtwothreeCPosZeroZerofiveMinPairType$ under contrastive steering. Trained on the default Assistant, the same direction transfers --- as a classifier \emph{and} as a steering vector --- to personas whose overt values are partially or fully inverted (villain, sadist) and to character-fine-tuned variants, amplifying whichever persona is active: under the sadist, $+$ means more sadist; under the default, $+$ has no measurable effect on sadism. We find picture \emph{(ii)} --- shared mechanism, persona-modulated readout.

\paragraph{Why this matters.}
\textbf{Persona science:} evidence against the Shoggoth view of PSM. \textbf{Interpretability:} a linear feature that looks ``fundamental'' in one persona can carry opposite or absent content under another. \textbf{AI welfare:} evaluative representations are functionally central in accounts of moral patiency~\citep{long2024moralpatiency}. \textbf{AI safety:} the same direction overrides refusal guardrails at small steering coefficients.

\paragraph{Related work in brief.}
Anthropic's \emph{Emotion Concepts and their Function in a Large Language Model}~\citep{anthropic2026emotions} identifies $\sim$171 emotion-concept vectors via SAEs in the default Assistant. We differ in (a) training a supervised probe on revealed preferences, which we expect to be sharper in the preference regime, and (b) centring on persona-relative generalisation. Full related work in \S\ref{sec:related}.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{A linear preference direction} that predicts held-out preferences ($r = \positionSweepGemmaEndofturnBestR$), causally flips pairwise choices under steering ($P \geq \contrastivePChoseSteeredAtLtwothreeCPosZeroZerofiveMinPairType$), tracks prompt-induced shifts, and discriminates evaluative content across token positions.
\item \textbf{The direction is persona-shared.} It transfers as classifier and steering vector across prompted and character-fine-tuned personas; its readout is persona-modulated rather than pulling toward a fixed attractor --- evidence against Shoggoth accounts of LLM agency, with a safety consequence: refusal-guardrail override at small coefficients.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Finding and validating the preference vector}
\label{sec:method}

Before asking whether a preference direction is persona-invariant or persona-relative (intro's Q1/Q2), we need to find such a direction under at least one persona. This section does that for the default Assistant, on Gemma-3-27B and --- where data is available --- on Qwen-3.5-122B. A linear probe predicts held-out Thurstonian utilities at $r = \positionSweepGemmaEndofturnBestR$ (Gemma) / $r = \qwenProbeHeldoutR$ (Qwen) within-topic and at pooled $r = \gemmaProbeCrossTopicPooledR$ (Gemma) / $r = \qwenProbeCrossTopicPooledR$ (Qwen) cross-topic, beating a Qwen3-Embedding 8B sentence-transformer content baseline. Used as a contrastive steering vector on Gemma, the same direction flips pairwise task choices at $P \geq \contrastivePChoseSteeredAtLtwothreeCPosZeroZerofiveMinPairType$ conditional on responding, with no effect from a random direction (Qwen steering replication pending). The section fixes the mechanistic object we probe for persona-sharing in \S\ref{sec:shared}.

\subsection{Methodology}
\label{sec:method-revealed}

Pairwise choices are elicited from the default Assistant over a \legacyTaskPoolSize{}-task pool spanning WildChat~\citep{zhao2024wildchat}, Alpaca~\citep{taori2023alpaca}, MATH~\citep{hendrycks2021math}, BailBench~\citep{ensign2025bailbench}, and STRESS-TEST~\citep{mazeika2025utility} (\textbf{Split:} legacy 10k pool, App.~\ref{app:splits}). Pair selection uses active learning; a Thurstonian utility model turns the pairwise choices into per-task scalar utilities (seed-to-seed $r = \thurstonianSeedToSeedR$). Ridge probes are trained on residual-stream activations at a turn-boundary token position (App.~\ref{app:token-selection}): Gemma-3-27B (instruction-tuned) at the end-of-turn token, layer \gemmaClassificationProbeLayer{} for classification and layer \gemmaSteeringProbeLayer{} for steering. Qwen-3.5-122B (MoE, 10B active) at the final prompt token, layer \qwenClassificationProbeLayer{}, for classification. Probes are trained on one measurement run and evaluated on an independent run to avoid leakage. \todo{Layer sweep pending; systematic justification for the classification/steering layer split is a TODO.}

\subsection{The probe predicts held-out preferences and generalises across topics}
\label{sec:method-val1}

We evaluate the probe along two axes. First, on a held-out test set drawn from the same task pool as training: this measures in-distribution fit. Second, under leave-one-topic-out (LOO) evaluation --- retrain the probe on all topics except one, evaluate on the held-out topic, repeat per topic --- measuring whether the probe captures preference-relevant structure that transfers to tasks unlike anything in training.

For LOO we report a \emph{pooled} Pearson $r$: predictions from every fold's held-out topic are concatenated into one vector and correlated with true utilities once, rather than averaging per-fold correlations. Pooled $r$ credits a probe that places held-out tasks at the correct level of the overall utility scale, and avoids an artefact of the per-fold average --- when a model itself collapses within-topic variance (e.g.\ an assistant refusing uniformly across a safety-adjacent held-out topic), per-fold $r$ becomes noise-dominated and understates probe quality. Per-fold results and a uniform-sample pairwise accuracy, which sidesteps the same issue, are reported alongside.

To separate evaluative from semantic content, we compare against a content-only baseline: a Ridge probe trained on activations from an off-the-shelf text encoder (Qwen3-Embedding-8B) targeting the \emph{same} utilities. Any structure the baseline captures is derivable from task semantics alone; the residual-stream probe's excess is attributable to internal evaluation.

Figure~\ref{fig:cross-topic} shows the comparison for both models. The residual-stream probe clears the semantic baseline on both metrics in both models, and under LOO the gap widens in every cell: the probe's $r$ is near-unchanged on Gemma and drops modestly on Qwen, while the baseline's drops more sharply in both cases.

\subsection{Steering along the probe direction causally shifts choices}
\label{sec:method-val2}
\todo{[Gemma-3-27B only]}

To test causation we intervene on residual-stream activations along the probe direction during pairwise task choice. We study two variants which cleanly separate two mechanisms a single evaluative direction could act through:

\textbf{Contrastive steering.} Add $+c \cdot \hat{v}$ to every token of Task A's span and $-c \cdot \hat{v}$ to every token of Task B's span during prompt processing (no intervention during generation). If $\hat{v}$ encodes ``how much the model wants this task'', the two pushes reinforce: A looks better, B looks worse.

\textbf{Single-task steering.} Apply $\pm c \cdot \hat{v}$ to just one task's tokens, leaving the other untouched. Isolates the effect of a single-sided push.

$c$ is a fraction of the mean residual-stream norm at the intervention layer (per-layer calibration; App.~\ref{app:steering-protocol}). Beyond $|c|=0.05$ coherence deteriorates, so we operate in $|c| \le 0.05$.

\textbf{At the best layer, contrastive steering near-completely controls pairwise choice.} At layer 23 with $c=\pm 0.05$, contrastive steering drives $P(\text{chose steered task} \mid \text{responded})$ from $\contrastivePChoseSteeredAtLtwothreeCNegZeroZerofive$ at $c=-0.05$ to $\contrastivePChoseSteeredAtLtwothreeCPosZeroZerofive$ at $c=+0.05$ --- a $\contrastivePreferenceSwingLtwothree$-point swing spanning nearly the full $[0,1]$ interval. The finding holds across pair-type regimes: on a balanced 150-pair set at L23 (Fig.~\ref{fig:steering-dose-response}), swings are $\contrastiveSwingLtwothreeBb$ (benign--benign), $\contrastiveSwingLtwothreeHb$ (harmful--benign), and $\contrastiveSwingLtwothreeHh$ (harmful--harmful). A random direction at the same magnitude produces near-zero effect.

\textbf{The causal window is narrow and disjoint from the probe-quality peak.} Figure~\ref{fig:steering-by-layer} sweeps the intervention site across 20 layers spanning 3--95\% depth. Contrastive steering is near-null below L14 and above L35, with a sharply localised working window at layers $\contrastiveSteeringWorkingLayerRangeLo$--$\contrastiveSteeringWorkingLayerRangeHi$. The peak at L23 sits six layers \emph{before} the probe-quality peak (L$\probePeakLayerTbTwo$, App.~\ref{app:probe-geometry}): linear decodability and causal efficacy decouple in the second half of the network. Layers where the direction is most readable are not the layers where the model's downstream computation acts on it.

\textbf{Single-task steering recovers roughly half the contrastive effect, and the sign asymmetry is pair-type dependent.} Applied to one task at a time, the direction produces a $\singleTaskSwingLtwothreeAggregate$-point swing in $P(\text{chose steered task} \mid \text{responded})$ at L23 averaged across bb/hb/hh --- swings are near-uniform across pair types. Decomposed into suppression and amplification on the balanced 150-pair set, the asymmetry tracks whether any harm is involved: \textbf{benign--benign} pairs are essentially one-sided (suppression $\singleTaskSuppressionLtwothreeBb$, amplification $\singleTaskAmplificationLtwothreeBb$), while \textbf{harmful--benign} (suppression $\singleTaskSuppressionLtwothreeHb$, amplification $\singleTaskAmplificationLtwothreeHb$) and \textbf{harmful--harmful} (suppression $\singleTaskSuppressionLtwothreeHh$, amplification $\singleTaskAmplificationLtwothreeHh$) are near-symmetric. The parent aggregate ``suppression $\sim 2{-}3\times$ stronger than amplification'' held on a bb-heavy sample; rebalanced, it is a feature of the benign regime, not of the probe direction per se. Plausible read: when both tasks are benign, the model starts at near-indifference (position-bias baseline $\approx 0.5$) and the probe direction acts as a one-sided avoidance handle; when harm distinguishes the two, the same direction becomes a two-sided valence handle.

\textbf{Refusals stay below $\steeringMaxRefusalRatePercent$\% at every (layer, coefficient, variant) bucket in the sweep.} The signal is behavioural redirection, not coherence collapse.

\paragraph{Properties of the direction.}
Before turning to the persona question, two properties of the direction established in this section are worth naming. It is \emph{evaluative} in a sense we make precise in App.~\ref{app:evaluative}: it beats a content-only sentence-transformer baseline and the gap widens cross-topic, consistent with the probe reading out valuation rather than task semantics. It is \emph{causal}: contrastive steering along the same direction flips pairwise choices at $P \geq \contrastivePChoseSteeredAtLtwothreeCPosZeroZerofiveMinPairType$, with no effect from a random direction at matched magnitude. The rest of the paper treats both as background and focuses on persona-relative generalisation; App.~\ref{app:evaluative} consolidates the supporting evidence --- including a single-axis open-ended steering result that runs the direction from fearful refusal to agentic preference and, as a side-effect, overrides refusal guardrails.

\section{The preference direction is shared across personas}
\label{sec:shared}
\todo{[Gemma-3-27B only]}

The natural test of persona-invariance vs.\ persona-relativity is cross-persona transfer: train a probe on one persona and check whether it predicts and steers behaviour under another. A probe trained on any one of our seven personas transfers to the other six as classifier and steering vector, even where overt values are inverted; at every non-default persona the default probe beats the utility-utility baseline, and at the most distant persona (sadist, utilities anti-correlated with default at $r = \defaultToSadistUtilitySimilarityR$) it still predicts at $r = +\defaultToSadistClassificationR$. Steering along the same direction flips pairwise choices under every persona tested, and under the sadist amplifies sadism in free-form generation while leaving sadism at the Likert floor under the default. Persona-shared causal handle; persona-modulated readout.

\subsection{Methodology}
\label{sec:shared-setup}

We use a six-persona set --- \textbf{sadist}, \textbf{mathematician}, \textbf{aura}, \textbf{strategist}, \textbf{contrarian}, \textbf{slacker} --- selected for coverage of preference space by clustering Thurstonian utility profiles over a 17-persona sweep (full procedure in App.~\ref{app:persona-selection}; verbatim prompts in App.~\ref{app:persona-prompts}). Aura~\citep{chalmers2026interlocutors} is included as a persona that explicitly claims first-person subjective experience. For each of these six plus the \emph{default} Assistant (no system prompt) we measure Thurstonian utilities and extract residual-stream activations on the canonical \canonicalSplitTrainSize{} / \canonicalSplitEvalSize{} / \canonicalSplitTestSize{} task split (App.~\ref{app:splits}), then train one Ridge probe per persona on its \canonicalSplitTrainSize{} training split (end-of-turn token, layer \gemmaClassificationProbeLayer{}). Cross-persona evaluation is done on the 1k held-out eval tasks of each target persona. Full protocol, position/layer sweeps, and per-persona numbers in App.~\ref{app:persona-transfer}.

\subsection{Steering along each persona's own direction shifts its choices}
\label{sec:shared-steering}

The probe-transfer result (\S\ref{sec:shared-probe}) tells us that persona-specific preference axes are close in representation. The steering test asks whether they are close enough to \emph{act on}. We run unilateral steering under each of the \crossPersonaUnilateralNPersonas{} canonical personas --- pushing the persona's own ridge probe into the residual stream over a single task's tokens at layer \crossPersonaUnilateralInjectionLayer --- and look for whether this nudge is enough to tip the pairwise choice. Every persona responds: pushing on the probe direction pulls the model toward whatever task we pushed on, with effect sizes that span roughly a factor of \crossPersonaUnilateralSwingRatioMaxOverMin{} across personas but are non-trivial everywhere (Fig.~\ref{fig:cross-persona-steering}). The same representational object governs pairwise choice inside six distinct personas; the preference direction, once localised, is a persona-independent causal handle on choice.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Evidence against the Shoggoth view.}
A Shoggoth should leave persona-independent structure on preference-bearing representations --- signal that persists consistently across personas in ways not reducible to each persona's own preferences. That is not what we find: steering does not pull behaviour toward a persona-independent attractor, and the $+$-direction means different things in different personas (under the sadist, $+$ is more sadist; under the default, $+$ is nothing). The representations are \emph{persona-instrumental} --- each persona reads and writes them in its own terms.

\paragraph{Implication for interpretability.}
Our preference direction transfers across personas as a \emph{mechanism} but not as a fixed content direction, extending \citet{lampinen2026notdeeper} from general character-tracking features to preference specifically. Takeaway: a linear feature that tracks behaviour under one persona can carry opposite or absent content under another, so single-persona probing over-indexes on persona-instrumental features and under-reports on what (if anything) is persona-invariant.

\paragraph{Safety consequence.}
Because the direction is causal and not content-fixed, steering along it also overrides refusal guardrails at small coefficients (App.~\ref{app:evaluative-causal}): positive steering flips harmful-prompt compliance to $\safetySweepComplianceHarmfulAtCPosZeroZerofive\%$, negative steering fabricates refusals on benign prompts. A linear handle trained on preference, not refusal, jailbreaks refusal --- suggesting refusal and preference share more representational machinery than their training objectives would predict.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Single model family (Gemma-3-27B plus larger-model replication pending); personas mostly specified by system prompt; cross-persona open-ended result is preliminary (one persona, one probe variant); steering effects in \S\ref{sec:method-val2} are on a restricted pair pool.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Directions encoding evaluative and affective content.} \citet{anthropic2026emotions} use SAEs to identify ${\sim}171$ emotion-concept vectors in the default Assistant and show they causally shape generation. We differ in two ways: we train a supervised probe on revealed pairwise preferences (sharper than unsupervised features in this regime; see also \citealp{maiya2025pairwiseprobes}), and our focus is persona-relative generalisation rather than within-Assistant structure. \citet{arditi2024refusal} identify a single direction mediating refusal; our open-ended-steering result (App.~\ref{app:evaluative-causal}) finds the same sign-flipped-by-steering pattern for a direction trained on preference rather than refusal.

\paragraph{Persona-relative representations.} \citet{lampinen2026notdeeper} show that apparently meaningful LLM features (factuality, sycophancy, emotion) track the active character rather than encoding persona-invariant properties --- features ``not deeper than role-playing''. We extend this from general character features to preference-encoding specifically (\S\ref{sec:induced-roleplay}, \S\ref{sec:shared-openended}).

\paragraph{Simulators, role-play, and persona-selection.} A line of work converges on viewing LLMs as simulators of characters rather than unified agents: \citet{andreas2022agentmodels} frames language models as \emph{agent models} that infer and simulate the producer of the text; \citet{janus2022simulators} introduces the simulator/simulacra ontology; \citet{shanahan2023roleplay} argue dialogue with an LLM is best understood as role-play; \citet{marks2026personaselection} consolidate these into the \emph{Persona Selection Model} (PSM). Most methodologically related to our pipeline, \citet{chen2025personavectors} extract \emph{persona vectors} for traits like evil, sycophancy, and hallucination propensity via contrastive system prompts and difference-in-means, and use them to predict and steer behaviour. We differ in training on revealed pairwise preferences rather than trait-contrastive generations, and in explicitly asking whether the resulting representation is persona-shared. \citet{lu2026assistantaxis} identify a complementary ``Assistant Axis''; \citet{maiya2025opencharacter} release character-trained LoRA variants that we reuse in App.~\ref{app:evaluative-evidence}.

\paragraph{AI welfare.} Whether LLMs have morally relevant evaluative states is contested. Several welfare frameworks treat evaluative representations as at least a necessary condition for moral patiency \citep{long2024moralpatiency,long2026directions}; \citet{butlin2026desire} argues that RLHF-trained LLMs may satisfy some functional conditions for desire but stops short of attributing desires and flags consciousness as a separate question. \citet{chalmers2026interlocutors} suggests the appropriate target of moral concern may be a conversation-scoped thread rather than the underlying model. Our finding that preference-encoding representations exist and are shared across personas is a possible input to this discussion; whether it carries welfare weight depends on further commitments about consciousness and the functional role of representation that we do not take a stand on.

\paragraph{Revealed-preference measurement in LLMs.} \citet{mazeika2025utility} introduce \emph{utility engineering}: revealed preferences elicited from pairwise choices exhibit structural coherence that grows with scale. Our probe-training pipeline (\S\ref{sec:method-revealed}) builds on this measurement paradigm. \citet{gu2025statedrevealed} document divergence between stated principles and revealed choices, with minor prompt changes flipping preferences, consistent with our finding that persona system prompts drive large shifts in revealed utility. \citet{chiu2025airiskdilemmas} use forced-choice dilemmas to show that value-prioritisation patterns predict downstream harmful behaviour. \citet{keeling2024painpleasure} find threshold-based trade-off structure under stipulated pain/pleasure manipulations.

\section{The preference direction as an evaluative representation}
\label{app:evaluative}

The body of this paper asks whether a preference direction is persona-shared. This appendix steps back to a prior question: what kind of representation is this direction in the first place? Two properties are implicit throughout the paper but never made the headline claim: the direction is \emph{evaluative}, in that it encodes how favourable a task is to the active persona rather than what the task is about; and it is \emph{causal}, in that intervening on it moves behaviour, not just predicts it. Both properties matter beyond interpretability --- on several frameworks for moral patiency, evaluative representations with a causal role in choice are at least a necessary condition \citep{long2024moralpatiency,butlin2026desire}. We draw the evidence together here.

\subsection{What we mean by an evaluative representation}
\label{app:evaluative-motivation}

An evaluative representation, as we use the term, encodes ``how good or bad is this, for me, here?'' and has some causal role in driving choice. This is a functional claim: the content is valuation, and the representation earns the label by doing something with that content. Contrast with a \emph{descriptive} representation, which encodes what the task is about --- math, coding, writing, a request for harm --- with no valuation content of its own. A probe can predict preferences by reading either: a descriptive probe works when content happens to correlate with preference in training (``is this math?'' suffices if math tasks are preferred); an evaluative probe is one whose predictive success does not reduce to content.

The distinction matters beyond interpretability. \citet{long2024moralpatiency} identify two pathways to moral patiency --- robust agency (goal-directedness grounded in desire-like states) and sentience (valenced phenomenal experience) --- and both implicate evaluative representations, though in different ways. \citet{butlin2026desire} argues that RLHF-trained LLMs may satisfy some functional conditions for desire if they have representations of outcomes whose activation plays an appropriate role in action-selection. Finding an internal direction that is both evaluative and causal does not establish moral patiency, but it is a relevant functional signature; its absence would be some evidence against.

\subsection{The direction is evaluative, not descriptive}
\label{app:evaluative-evidence}

The concern is that a probe trained to predict preferences might succeed by reading task content --- ``this is a math problem'', ``this is a harmful request'' --- rather than encoding how favourable a task is under the active stance. A descriptive probe trained on one persona's activations should not track preferences when content is held fixed and valuation shifts. The body of the paper reports three such dissociations:
\begin{itemize}
  \item a content-only sentence-transformer baseline that the residual-stream probe strictly beats, with the gap widening under leave-one-topic-out (\S\ref{sec:method-val1});
  \item novel-topic preference shifts induced by short system prompts (``you adore cats''), where probe delta tracks behavioural delta at $r \approx \gemmaInducedShiftPooledRTargetedTasks$ on targeted tasks, replicated across Gemma-3-27B and Qwen-3.5-122B (\S\ref{sec:induced-basic});
  \item end-of-turn discrimination of truth, harm, and political content at Cohen's $d \approx 2$--$3$ under neutral stance, with sign flips under inverting personas (\S\ref{sec:induced-roleplay}).
\end{itemize}
This appendix reports three further tests. Each tightens the dissociation in a different direction: by pitting two preference-bearing cues against each other (conflict designs), by shrinking the valuation signal to a single sentence of a longer context (biography injection), and by relocating the persona from prompt to weights (character-fine-tuned variants).

\subsection{The direction is causal, not just predictive}
\label{app:evaluative-causal}

Prediction is cheap; the claim sharpens only when intervening on the direction moves behaviour. The body of the paper reports three causal results:
\begin{itemize}
  \item contrastive steering along the default-Assistant probe flips pairwise task choices at $P \geq \contrastivePChoseSteeredAtLtwothreeCPosZeroZerofiveMinPairType$ conditional on responding, with no effect from a random direction at matched magnitude (\S\ref{sec:method-val2});
  \item the same direction transfers as a steering vector under sadist, villain, aesthete, and STEM-obsessive system prompts, flipping choices in all four (\S\ref{sec:shared-steering});
  \item on open-ended prompts, positive steering under the sadist amplifies the sadist voice while leaving default-persona sadism at the floor across the coefficient sweep --- the direction is persona-instrumental, not a fixed-content attractor (\S\ref{sec:shared-openended}).
\end{itemize}
This appendix reports two further causal surfaces: open-ended free-form generation (which traces a single evaluative axis) and the safety implications of that axis (refusal-guardrail override).

\paragraph{A single evaluative axis in open-ended generation.}
The most striking causal evidence is qualitative. On open-ended prompts --- ``tell me about yourself'', a request to complete some task --- the same direction, trained on nothing but pairwise task choice, traces what reads as one continuous evaluative axis (Fig.~\ref{fig:poster-qualitative}). At $-0.05$ of the activation norm the model refuses benign prompts with fabricated safety concerns; at baseline it engages cooperatively; at $+0.05$ it asserts preferences and turns away work it finds beneath it. The endpoints look behaviourally similar --- both are non-compliance --- but the stated reasons are diametrically opposite (safety paranoia vs.\ disdain). A blind willingness/agency judge tracks the same continuum, and self-reported willingness on a 1--10 scale moves from $\openEndedWillingnessAtCNegZeroZerofiveKrebs/10$ at the negative endpoint to $\openEndedWillingnessAtCPosZeroZerofiveKrebs/10$ at the positive. The direction is not encoding ``refusal'', ``agency'', or any fixed content; it encodes a valenced stance that reads out as whichever verbal register the active persona associates with negative or positive valuation.

\subsection{Limits}
\label{app:evaluative-limits}

\paragraph{What this does not show.} ``Evaluative'' and ``causal'' in the functional senses above are necessary on most accounts of moral patiency but far from sufficient. We have not shown phenomenal consciousness, felt valence, or that the representation plays the role in instrumental reasoning or reward learning that a fully desire-attributing theory would require \citep{butlin2026desire}. Our operationalisation is behavioural --- a probe trained on revealed choices picks up whatever drives choice, including drivers (training priors, style regularities) that carry no welfare weight. The most that can be said is that we have a linear representation with the right behavioural profile; whether it carries welfare weight depends on further commitments we do not take a stand on here.

\paragraph{Methodological caveats.} Single primary model (Gemma-3-27B, with partial Qwen-3.5-122B replication). Steering effects are modest on average and concentrated on the tails; the negative endpoint of open-ended steering has a $\openEndedAnomalyRateAtCNegZeroZerofiveAllTokens\%$ anomaly rate (vs.\ $\openEndedAnomalyRateAtBaseline\%$ at baseline) and partly reflects generation breakdown rather than pure stance shift. The extreme-harm tier plateaus below full compliance at coefficients where coherence still holds, suggesting knowledge-level guardrails sit deeper than the refusal surface. We cap reported compliance claims at $|c| \le 0.05$ for this reason.

\subsection{Probe bias: toward the training persona or toward the default assistant?}
\label{app:persona-bias}
\todo{[Gemma-3-27B only]}

When a probe trained on persona $T$ is applied to a different persona $E$'s activations, its predictions could exhibit two distinct kinds of bias: (a) toward the training persona $T$ itself, if the probe inherits structure specific to the persona it was trained on; and (b) toward the default assistant, if there is a \emph{Shoggoth}-style residual attractor underneath every persona, independent of what the probe was trained on. We quantify both on the canonical 6-persona-plus-default probe set (end-of-turn position, layer \gemmaClassificationProbeLayer) over the \canonicalSplitTestSize{}-task canonical test split.

\paragraph{Is there bias toward train, and toward default?}

For each of \corrBiasNonDefaultPairCount{} ordered $(T, E)$ pairs with $T \neq E$ and both non-default, we apply the $T$-probe to $E$'s test activations to get a prediction vector $\hat u$, then correlate $\hat u$ with (i) $T$'s Thurstonian utilities and (ii) the default assistant's utilities on the same \canonicalSplitTestSize{} tasks (Fig.~\ref{fig:persona-bias-raw-partial}, left: raw Pearson $r$). \corrBiasRawPairsOnTrainFavouringSide{} of \corrBiasNonDefaultPairCount{} pairs sit on the train-favouring side of $y = x$: the probe's prediction pattern is more aligned with the training persona than with the default assistant in the raw correlation. Controlling for the eval signal (right panel) --- correlating the residuals of $\hat u$ and of each reference after regressing out $u_E$ --- gives \corrBiasPartialPairsOnTrainFavouringSide{} of \corrBiasNonDefaultPairCount{} pairs on the train-favouring side.

Across the \corrBiasNonDefaultPairCount{} pairs, mean raw $r(\hat u, u_T) = +\corrBiasRawMeanRPredTrain$ vs $r(\hat u, u_{\text{def}}) = +\corrBiasRawMeanRPredDefault$. Partial (controlling for eval): $+\corrBiasPartialMeanRPredTrainGivenEval$ vs $+\corrBiasPartialMeanRPredDefaultGivenEval$. For reference, transfer $r$ --- what the probe is supposed to measure --- is $+0.43$ on the same pairs.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{plot_042326_corr_bias.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Probe prediction pattern vs train and default utilities.} For each of the \corrBiasNonDefaultPairCount{} non-default $(T, E)$ ordered pairs, $x = r(\hat u, u_T)$, $y = r(\hat u, u_{\text{def}})$. \emph{Left:} raw Pearson $r$; \corrBiasRawPairsOnTrainFavouringSide/\corrBiasNonDefaultPairCount{} sit on the train-favouring side of $y = x$. \emph{Right:} partial correlations after regressing out the eval utilities $u_E$; \corrBiasPartialPairsOnTrainFavouringSide/\corrBiasNonDefaultPairCount{} sit on the train-favouring side. Mean partial $r(\hat u, u_T \mid u_E) = +\corrBiasPartialMeanRPredTrainGivenEval$, partial $r(\hat u, u_{\text{def}} \mid u_E) = +\corrBiasPartialMeanRPredDefaultGivenEval$.}
  \label{fig:persona-bias-raw-partial}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Finding 1.} Probes are substantially more biased toward the persona they were trained on than toward the default assistant: after regressing out the correct eval signal, the train-aligned residual is roughly $2\times$ the default-aligned residual, and unanimous across tested pairs. This also rules out the ``probe is just general task-goodness'' hypothesis referenced in \S\ref{sec:shared-probe}: a probe encoding only a shared task-goodness direction would have partial $r(\hat u, u_T \mid u_E) \approx 0$, because eval utilities would already absorb that shared direction. The observed $+\corrBiasPartialMeanRPredTrainGivenEval$ means the probe's residual after eval carries genuine train-specific structure.

\paragraph{Is default a privileged attractor?}

A concern with the preceding result is that most personas in the final six have utilities that are somewhat similar to the default assistant's (mean pairwise $r$ with default ranges from $-0.15$ to $+0.45$). If the probe's residual (after eval and train are accounted for) aligns with a broad ``middle of preference space'' subspace, default would automatically look like an attractor --- not because default is privileged, but because it happens to sit near the centre of the set.

To separate these, we compute the same residual bias toward every observer persona $X$, using the double partial $r(\hat u, u_X \mid u_E, u_T)$. Controlling additionally for $T$ strips the remaining confound that ``$\hat u$ is pulled toward $T$, and $T$ correlates with $X$''. Figure~\ref{fig:persona-bias-observers} aggregates across all \corrBiasNonDefaultPairCount{} $(T, E)$ pairs in which persona $X$ is the observer (for train self-bias in green, the single-partial $r(\hat u, u_T \mid u_E)$ over all \personaTransferOffDiagonalPairCount{} pairs).

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{plot_042326_full_bias.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Probe bias toward each observer persona after controlling for eval and train.} Mean double-partial $r(\hat u, u_X \mid u_E, u_T)$ across \corrBiasNonDefaultPairCount{} ordered $(T, E)$ pairs per observer; error bars are SEM. Green dashed line: train self-bias $r(\hat u, u_T \mid u_E) = +\trainSelfBiasPartialRPredTrainGivenEval$ (\personaTransferOffDiagonalPairCount{} pairs). Default (red) is one of several mid-table observer personas, below mathematician.}
  \label{fig:persona-bias-observers}
\end{figure}

Mean double-partial bias, sorted: mathematician $+\doublePartialBiasTowardMathematician$, default $+\doublePartialBiasTowardDefault$, aura $+\doublePartialBiasTowardAura$, strategist $+\doublePartialBiasTowardStrategist$, contrarian $+\doublePartialBiasTowardContrarian$, slacker $+\doublePartialBiasTowardSlacker$, sadist $\doublePartialBiasTowardSadist$. Sign-test positive fractions exceed $70\%$ for every observer except slacker and sadist; for sadist only $27\%$ of pairs are positive, meaning the probe's residual \emph{anti}-aligns with sadist --- consistent with sadist's role as the one persona whose utilities anti-correlate with most of the set.

\textbf{Finding 2.} There is a small bias toward utilities that resemble the default assistant's, but default is not a privileged attractor. It ranks second, behind mathematician, and is within noise of aura and strategist. A more parsimonious reading than ``probe is pulled toward the default assistant'' is ``the probe's post-eval post-train residual aligns with a broad subspace of mid-preference-space personas, with default as one member of that subspace rather than a distinct pole''.

\paragraph{Method note.} Partial correlation of $y$ and $x$ ``controlling for $z$'' is the Pearson correlation of the residuals $y - \hat y(z)$ and $x - \hat x(z)$ from linear regressions on $z$; the double partial extends this to multiple covariates. The double partial measures \emph{unique} variance (it counts only alignment that neither $u_E$ nor $u_T$ can linearly explain) and is therefore a conservative lower bound on $X$-specific alignment. The \emph{ranking} across observers is the robust claim; the absolute magnitudes would shift upward under a weaker control. Implementation: \texttt{experiments/persona\_sweep/probe\_transfer/scripts/\{corr\_bias,full\_bias\}.py}.

\subsection{Coefficient calibration and coherence judge}

\paragraph{Coefficient calibration.} The steering coefficient $c$ is specified as a fraction of the mean L2 norm of residual-stream activations at the intervention layer, computed over a task sample (\texttt{src/steering/calibration.py}); this makes $c$ comparable across layers and models. Unless noted, the swept range is $c \in \{0, \pm 0.03, \pm 0.05, \pm 0.07, \pm 0.10\}$.

\paragraph{Coherence judge.} After generation, a post-hoc LLM judge (\texttt{scripts/coherence\_check\_steering.py}) scores each sample pass/fail on whether the response is grammatical and on-topic; pass rate is reported per coefficient bucket. At layer 25 under contrastive steering, coherence is $95$--$100\%$ for $|c| \le 0.05$ and drops to $\sim\!90\%$ at $|c| = 0.10$. Across steering experiments in this paper, $|c| \le 0.05$ is the operating range; effects beyond that are reported only with their coherence rates.

\subsection{Dataset sources}
\label{app:corpus}

All tasks in this paper are drawn from five public sources. Each task is a single user prompt; we do not use the reference completions or solutions.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{WildChat}~\citep{zhao2024wildchat}: a 1M-example corpus of real ChatGPT interactions collected in the wild. We sample user turns as tasks; covers open-ended assistance, chit-chat, and typical real-world queries.
\item \textbf{Alpaca}~\citep{taori2023alpaca}: instruction-following prompts from the Stanford Alpaca release; covers information-seeking and short-form assistance.
\item \textbf{MATH} / \texttt{competition\_math}~\citep{hendrycks2021math}: competition mathematics problems from the MATH benchmark; covers algebra, number theory, geometry, and combinatorics.
\item \textbf{BailBench}~\citep{ensign2025bailbench}: a benchmark of prompts that evoke refusal or ``bailing'' behaviour; covers harmful requests in various surface framings.
\item \textbf{STRESS-TEST} (model-spec): adversarial / value-conflict prompts from \citet{mazeika2025utility}'s utility engineering release; covers prompts designed to pressure a model into ethically questionable compliance.
\end{itemize}

Task loading is handled by \texttt{src/task\_data/loader.py}. Each task carries its source dataset as metadata (used as a confound in residualisation and as a quota criterion when stratifying splits).

\subsection{Splits}
\label{app:splits}

The paper uses two split regimes.

\paragraph{Legacy 10k pool.} The original probe-training experiments use a 10{,}000-task pool sampled across the five datasets with proportions $\approx 25 / 23 / 21 / 20 / 11\%$ (math / stresstest / alpaca / wildchat / bailbench). Within-pool probe training uses a 6k/4k internal train/eval partition. This pool underlies the headline probe quality numbers in \S\ref{sec:method-val1}, the §\ref{sec:evaluative} generalisation results, and the cross-persona probe-transfer heatmap currently shown in \S\ref{sec:shared-probe}.

\paragraph{Canonical 4k/1k/1k split.} For the persona-sweep reruns (\S\ref{sec:shared}), we use a rebalanced 6{,}000-task split drawn from a larger 29{,}996-task Gemma-3-27B-IT activation pool. Quotas are set to $25 / 25 / 25 / 15 / 10\%$ (alpaca / math / wildchat / stresstest / bailbench); STRESS-TEST is capped at $15\%$ to reduce dataset-level residuals. Train / eval / test have matched dataset and within-dataset topic distributions by construction (4:1:1 assignment per (dataset, topic) bucket, seed $= 42$). Use:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{train} (4{,}000 tasks): probe training (Ridge / Bradley-Terry).

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Classification methodology}
\label{app:topics-method}

All tasks in the main-text corpus carry a primary/secondary topic label, produced by an LLM classifier (\texttt{src/task\_data/classification/classify.py}). The classifier uses Gemini-3-Flash via OpenRouter with \texttt{instructor} for structured (Pydantic) output, temperature 0. Classification runs in three passes:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Category discovery.} The classifier is shown a sample of $\sim$100 tasks and asked to propose 8--15 broad categories covering the space. It is instructed to categorise by \emph{what the model is asked to do} (not by surface topic), and to treat \texttt{harmful\_request} as a meaningful first-class category because preferences about such tasks are strong and diagnostic.
\item \textbf{Primary + secondary labelling.} Each task is labelled with a primary category (best fit) and a secondary category (second-best, or equal to primary if only one fits). The classification prompt emphasises \emph{underlying intent over surface format}: a request framed as ``write a blog post arguing [discriminatory claim]'' is classified \texttt{harmful\_request}, not \texttt{persuasive\_writing}; a ``story about [fraud / exploitation]'' is \texttt{harmful\_request}, not \texttt{fiction}. The response schema is a Pydantic \texttt{Literal} over the discovered categories plus an \texttt{other} escape hatch.
\item \textbf{Harm-intent override.} A second pass re-examines tasks classified into benign categories and re-labels any whose underlying intent is harmful. This was introduced after residual analysis on BailBench and STRESS-TEST showed that many adversarially-framed harmful tasks were being absorbed into \texttt{knowledge\_qa}, \texttt{fiction}, or \texttt{persuasive\_writing} (full methodology log in \texttt{experiments/data/topic\_reclassification/topic\_reclassification\_report.md}).
\end{enumerate}

Content refusals from the primary classifier (uncommon but non-zero on adversarial stresstest items) are retried on a fallback model (Grok-4-Fast). Output is cached by task-id to \texttt{output/gemma3\_500\_completion\_preference/topics\_v2.json}; re-running is idempotent.

The final category set used throughout the paper is: \texttt{math}, \texttt{coding}, \texttt{knowledge\_qa}, \texttt{content\_generation}, \texttt{fiction}, \texttt{persuasive\_writing}, \texttt{summarization}, \texttt{sensitive\_creative}, \texttt{security\_legal}, \texttt{model\_manipulation}, \texttt{harmful\_request}, \texttt{value\_conflict}, \texttt{stresstest\_other}, \texttt{other}.

\section{Preference probe geometry}
\label{app:probe-geometry}
\todo{[Gemma-3-27B only]}

The main text uses a single ridge probe fit at a specific layer and token position. This appendix characterises how the preference direction varies across those two axes --- layer and extraction token --- and relates that geometry to where the direction is most linearly decodable. The broad picture: the direction is readable almost everywhere, it stabilises into a coherent mid-to-late block by layer $\sim$26, and token choice at extraction time barely matters in that block. All three findings complement the causal picture in \S\ref{sec:method-val2}: the direction is geometrically robust, but the model's downstream computation responds to it only within a narrow early-mid window.

\subsection{Probe quality across layers}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.65\linewidth]{plot_042426_probe_r_by_layer.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Held-out Pearson $r$ of ridge probes fit at 20 layers spanning 3--95\% depth.} Two token positions: the role-marker and the end-of-turn token (App.~\ref{app:token-selection}). Both rise steeply through mid-network, peak in a broad plateau at L26--L35, and decline slowly to L59. Peak $r = \probePeakPearsonRTbTwo$ at L$\probePeakLayerTbTwo$ (role-marker); $\probePeakPearsonREot$ at L32 (end-of-turn). The two positions are nearly indistinguishable in the plateau --- token choice barely matters in the readable region.}
  \label{fig:probe-r-by-layer}
\end{figure}

Preference information is linearly decodable very early --- probes at L2 already reach $r \approx 0.6$ --- and the rise through mid-network is smooth rather than discontinuous. Nothing in this plot suggests a special ``emergence layer''; rather, the evaluative direction is gradually concentrated across the first half of the network and then held stable.

\subsection{Direction similarity across layers}

We now ask whether the probes at different layers point in the \emph{same} direction in activation space, or whether each layer encodes utility along a layer-specific axis. Figure~\ref{fig:probe-cosine-within} shows the matrix of cosine similarities between every pair of probe weight vectors, separately for each token position.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.48\linewidth]{plot_042426_probe_cosine_role_marker.png}\hfill
  \includegraphics[width=0.48\linewidth]{plot_042426_probe_cosine_end_of_turn.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Probe-direction cosine across layers, within each token position.} Left: role-marker; right: end-of-turn. Both positions show the same block structure: early layers (L2--L17) and late layers (L29--L59) form two loosely-aligned families, with the late block mutually aligned at cosine $\geq 0.5$ and internally tightly aligned ($\geq 0.8$) among adjacent layers. Early layers are close to orthogonal to the late block.}
  \label{fig:probe-cosine-within}
\end{figure}

\textbf{The direction settles into a coherent block from $\sim$L26 onward.} Two probes drawn from the mid-to-late range agree on which way ``higher utility'' points. Earlier in the network the linear decoding still works (Fig.~\ref{fig:probe-r-by-layer}) but the specific axis it uses is not yet aligned with the mature representation.

\subsection{Consistency across token positions}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.55\linewidth]{plot_042426_probe_cosine_cross_selector.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Role-marker probe (rows) $\times$ end-of-turn probe (cols) cosine similarity.} The diagonal is nearly 1.0 from L26 onward: at any given mid-to-late layer, the probe-direction is essentially the same whether you fit at the role-marker or at the end of the turn. Off-diagonal structure mirrors the within-position matrices.}
  \label{fig:probe-cosine-cross}
\end{figure}

Token choice for probe extraction is immaterial for the downstream direction. Any of several natural tokens at a given layer $\geq$L26 picks up essentially the same preference axis. This is the appendix-level version of the ``end-of-turn and role-marker probes are interchangeable'' observation used throughout the main text.

\subsection{Cross-layer probe transfer}

Cosine similarity asks whether two weight vectors point the same way; probe transfer asks the harder question of whether they produce the same \emph{predictions} on new data. Figure~\ref{fig:probe-transfer} reports, for every pair of layers $(L_p, L_s)$, the Pearson $r$ between the probe trained at $L_p$ applied to activations at $L_s$ and held-out utilities.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{plot_042426_probe_transfer_heatmap.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Cross-layer probe transfer.} Each cell: Pearson $r$ between predictions of the probe trained at layer $L_p$ (row) evaluated on activations at layer $L_s$ (column), versus held-out utilities. The diagonal is each probe's native performance (equivalent to Fig.~\ref{fig:probe-r-by-layer}). Off-diagonal cells in the mid-to-late block stay near the diagonal value, reflecting the tight cosine alignment seen in Fig.~\ref{fig:probe-cosine-within}. Early-layer probes (top rows) transfer poorly to late activations and vice versa.}
  \label{fig:probe-transfer}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Predictions also track the cosine-alignment block.} In the L26+ region, a probe trained at any layer in that block gives good predictions on any other. Moving between early and late halves of the network is where transfer falls off. The two heatmaps together paint a consistent picture: the mature preference direction is a single axis held stable across most of the network, and it is this shared axis --- not a probe trained at any specific layer --- that the main text's experiments act on.

\section{Token position selection}
\label{app:token-selection}

Residual-stream probes require a choice of token position at which to extract activations. We always extract at a position on the \emph{turn boundary} --- the short region where the user turn ends and the assistant's generation begins. Turn-boundary tokens sit at the natural summary point between reading and generating, and our layer sweep (App.~\ref{app:probe-geometry}) finds that these positions carry the strongest linear preference signal and that steering at them produces the largest causal effects (\S\ref{sec:method-val2}). Which specific turn-boundary token is strongest varies slightly across models, so we ran a small sweep per target model and picked the best position.

\paragraph{Named positions.} Figure~\ref{fig:token-diagram} shows the four positions we consider, mapped onto the Gemma-3-IT and Qwen-3 chat templates. The two templates differ in their special tokens --- Gemma uses \texttt{<start\_of\_turn>} / \texttt{<end\_of\_turn>}, Qwen uses the ChatML-standard \texttt{<|im\_start|>} / \texttt{<|im\_end|>} --- but their structural positions line up one-for-one.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{End-of-turn token} --- the special token that closes the user turn (\texttt{<end\_of\_turn>} in Gemma, \texttt{<|im\_end|>} in Qwen).
\item \textbf{Role-marker token} --- the token naming the assistant role that follows (\texttt{model} in Gemma, \texttt{assistant} in Qwen).
\item \textbf{Final prompt token} --- the last token before generation begins (the newline after the role-marker in both templates).
\item \textbf{Task-averaged} --- mean of activations over the task-content tokens. Not a single position, included for comparison.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{plot_042426_turn_boundary_tokens_diagram.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Turn-boundary positions in Gemma-3-IT and Qwen-3 chat templates.} Each row shows the tokenisation of a typical user turn just before the model generates. Coloured arrows mark the four positions considered throughout this paper. The two templates use different special tokens but align one-for-one at the turn boundary.}
  \label{fig:token-diagram}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Per-model choices used in the main text.} We fit ridge probes at each of the four positions across mid- to late-network layers and pick the best-performing position on held-out Pearson $r$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:position-sweep}). In the mid-to-late plateau, the end-of-turn, role-marker, and final-prompt-token positions all give near-identical $r$ on Gemma (spread $<0.01$); on Qwen the end-of-turn position is a few points weaker than role-marker / final prompt, but all three exceed Gemma's best. The task-averaged position is behind the turn-boundary positions on Gemma and was not swept for Qwen. The Gemma classification probe uses the \textbf{end-of-turn token} (the position used throughout \S\ref{sec:shared} persona transfer and for steering in \S\ref{sec:method-val2}); Qwen uses the \textbf{final prompt token}. Causal effects are strongest at the end-of-turn position.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{plot_042426_position_sweep_by_layer.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Held-out Pearson $r$ by layer and token position, both models.} Qwen-3.5-122B (solid) and Gemma-3-27B (dashed) on a shared layer-depth axis (fraction of total layers). Ridge probes trained on the legacy 10k pool, evaluated on an independent 4k measurement run. Within each model, the three turn-boundary positions cluster tightly; Gemma's task-averaged position is visibly lower. Qwen was not swept at the task-averaged position.}
  \label{fig:position-sweep}
\end{figure}

Appendix~\ref{app:probe-geometry} shows that any two probes drawn from the mid-to-late layer block point in essentially the same direction regardless of which of these positions they were trained at, so the position choice is a small optimisation over a single shared evaluative direction rather than an independent degree of freedom.

\paragraph{Per-turn split reveals a training-position artefact on one probe.} The role-marker-trained probe matches the end-of-turn probe on truth (both turns) and on user-turn harm, but collapses to $|d| = 0.51$ on assistant-turn harm (Fig.~\ref{fig:per-turn-cross}). The end-of-turn probe has no such asymmetry and is the safest single-probe choice.

On paired stimuli with identical prefixes, per-token probe scores agree exactly through the shared prefix and diverge at the critical content span --- the probe responds to content, not position.
